the idea

見当Kentō — the mark that makes every impression land in the same place.

Before a colour is printed, the carver cuts kentō registration marks into the block: an L-shaped kagi at one corner and a straight hikitsuke along an edge. The paper is seated against them for every pass, so a dozen blocks land in perfect alignment — the first impression and the hundredth, framed exactly alike.

That is Hayao's promise, drawn from craft rather than jargon: same seed and build fix the frame → the same result, every run. Registration is determinism for software. The whole identity rests on that one honest rhyme — and the mark is a proof, not an ornament: wherever the seal appears, a verifiable claim appears with it.

the borrow

A mark of the hand, held to a machine's account.

A kentō is the trace of a human carver. Hayao is AI-first, so it does not claim the handwork — it claims the discipline. Determinism is what makes machine-made output accountable: every run reproducible, inspectable, and provable, the opposite of disposable generation. That is why this craft language fits, and the standard it holds itself to is written plainly in the duty of care below.

the mark

A registration mark — used identically everywhere.

The mark is the kentō itself: the vermilion kagi and hikitsuke holding the corner of an implied sheet. It is abstract and non-linguistic by design — a printer's tool, never a character — and it is drawn the same in every context, which literally enacts the idea it stands for.

wordmark

Mincho, set quietly, with room to breathe.

A single Mincho line — high-contrast, brush-cut strokes — with the mark to its left. On ink it reverses to washi and the vermilion warms for contrast. Clear space equals the height of the mark on every side.

colour

Traditional pigments, used with restraint.

Three inks and a paper. Vermilion (shu) is a seal, not a fill — one per view, on the mark and nowhere large. The core inks carry their historical Japanese names.

duty of care

Homage is earned, not worn.

This identity borrows a living craft. The same restraint that keeps it respectful is what keeps it elegant. Two rules do most of the work: credit the technique, and abstract, never counterfeit.

A launch gate — not a footnote

Do
  • Commission a paid review from a Japanese designer with authority to say no — before first public use.
  • Credit kentō and the ukiyo-e lineage on the about page.
  • Keep every mark abstract and non-linguistic.
  • Use real colour names accurately (藍 ai, 朱 shu, 墨 sumi).
Don't
  • Invent kanji, or set text in Japanese you can't read.
  • Use a kanji as a decorative UI glyph — the mark is abstract for this reason.
  • Claim exactness the artwork doesn't hold — it is seigaiha-inspired.
  • Imply any tie to a filmmaker or studio. The name is evocative, standalone, not an endorsement.
